```
Section 06
Price Chart — Technical Concepts
Fundamental analysis tells you WHAT to buy. Technical analysis tells you WHEN to buy. Every price term explained with a visual chart.
Annotated Stock Price Chart — Key Technical Concepts
```
Support Level
Price floor where buying demand appears
A price level where the stock has repeatedly bounced up. Think of it as a "floor". When price comes back to support with lower volume, it's often a buy zone. If support BREAKS — exit!
Resistance Level
Price ceiling where selling pressure appears
Where the stock repeatedly fails to go higher. Sellers dominate here. Once broken convincingly (with high volume), resistance becomes new support — a bullish signal.
Moving Averages (50/200 DMA)
Average of last 50 or 200 days' closing prices
Price above 200 DMA = long-term uptrend. 50 DMA crossing above 200 DMA = Golden Cross (bullish). 50 DMA crossing below 200 DMA = Death Cross (bearish).
Volume Analysis
Number of shares traded in a period
Price rise on HIGH volume = strong breakout (conviction). Price rise on LOW volume = weak move (may reverse). Always check: does the move have volume support?
Section 07
How to Value a Stock — Is it Cheap or Expensive?
Three main valuation methods used by professional analysts. Apply all three and triangulate.
1
Relative Valuation — P/E Comparison
Compare the stock's P/E to: (a) its own historical average P/E, (b) sector average P/E, (c) a direct competitor. If TCS historical average P/E is 28× and current is 27.4×, the stock is at fair to slight discount to its own history. Simple and most widely used.
2
Intrinsic Value — DCF (Discounted Cash Flow)
Project future cash flows for 5–10 years, then discount them back to today using a "discount rate" (usually 10–15%). The sum = intrinsic value per share. If intrinsic value > market price → undervalued. Very sensitive to assumptions. Used by fundamental investors (Buffett-style).
3
Asset-Based Valuation — P/B Ratio
If you wound up the company today, what's the net asset value per share? P/B <1 = buying assets cheaper than their book value. Most useful for banks, NBFCs, real estate, asset-heavy industries. Less relevant for asset-light IT/pharma companies.

Predicting Future Performance — What to Look For
No one can predict the future with certainty, but these frameworks help you form a probabilistic view of where a company is headed.
📈
Revenue Growth Trend
Is revenue growing consistently 10%+ per year? Accelerating growth (8% → 12% → 15%) is more bullish than decelerating (15% → 10% → 6%). Look at 5-year CAGR.
TCS: 12.4% 5Y CAGR — Bullish
💰
Margin Expansion
Are operating margins growing or shrinking? Expanding margins = better pricing power or cost efficiency = more profit per rupee of revenue.
TCS: Stable 24-26% OPM — Neutral
🏦
Free Cash Flow Generation
Consistent FCF positive + growing = the company is a cash machine. This funds dividends, buybacks, acquisitions. TCS FCF yield is among highest in Indian IT.
TCS: ₹44,000 Cr+ FCF — Very Bullish
🌍
Market & TAM Expansion
Is the company's addressable market growing? AI, cloud, digital transformation are growing TCS's opportunity. A shrinking TAM = structural headwind.
IT Services AI Boom — Bullish
⚠️
Competitive Moat
What stops competitors? Switching costs? Brand? Regulatory licences? Patents? Scale? A company with no moat gets disrupted. TCS has brand + scale + client lock-in.
Strong Brand Moat — Bullish
🎯
Management Quality
Read the Chairman's letter every year. Are they honest about challenges? Do they deliver on guidance? Check capital allocation history. Good management = compounding machine.
Tata Group Pedigree — Bullish
📊
Order Book / Backlog
For IT, infra, capital goods — the order book tells you FUTURE revenue visibility. A large and growing order book = revenue secured. This metric gives 12-18 months visibility.
₹67,000 Cr Order Book — Bullish
🔮
Analyst Consensus
What do 20–30 analysts covering the stock say? More BUY ratings with high target price upside = positive sentiment. But always do your own work — analysts can be wrong.
